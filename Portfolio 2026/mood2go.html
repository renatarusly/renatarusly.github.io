Case study • Mobile app

MOOD2GO

A mood-based place recommendation app that turns “Where should we go?” into a fast, guided decision. Built for the Google Maps Platform Hackathon (AI + Travel).

← Back to works

Overview

Role
UI/UX Product Designer • Back-end Contributor
Focus
User research • Interaction • Visual design • Testing
Outcome
Working prototype + API integration + final pitch

Problem

People want to go out, but end up stuck in long group debates—then choose the same familiar places to avoid conflict. The result is time wasted, decision fatigue, and less discovery.

Research insights

From casual observation across 10 outings with friends, I saw the same friction points repeat:

  • Indecision dominated planning: most groups debated for 15–20 minutes.
  • Repetitive destinations: 7 out of 10 groups ended up at the same spots.
  • Relief when suggestions arrived quickly: a fitting option often ended the debate instantly.

Solution

Mood2Go recommends places based on three inputs—mood, vibe, and distance—so users can choose quickly without scanning endless lists.

Insight → Decision

Users choose by vibe, not logic → we centered the flow around “mood” and “vibe,” not categories or rankings.

Iteration

Numbered mood inputs felt abstract → replaced with a swipe interaction and clear emotion labels.

UX principle

Keep momentum → a simple, quick flow from inputs → 3 recommendations → select → guide.

Results & takeaways

This was our first hackathon as a mostly-beginner team. We shipped a working prototype with coded features, API integration, and a polished UI—around 75% of the design vision implemented and demoed.

  • End-to-end execution: designed, built, and shipped under tight constraints.
  • Cross-disciplinary growth: I supported planning, testing recruitment, and back-end work.
  • Simplicity wins: fast, guided choices reduced debate and increased excitement during testing.

Future directions

  • Group mode: collaborative decision-making with friends.
  • Favorites: save and revisit go-to spots.
  • Memory-based recommendations: reduce repeats of recently visited places.
  • Expanded categories: richer moods, vibes, and more nuanced recommendations.
